Living Song
Lyric Composer Skill
Revised & Enhanced Edition


Purpose
This skill transforms family memory responses, songwriter's briefs, or intake form answers into complete, emotionally resonant song lyrics for memorial, hospice, and living tribute contexts. The output is always a full song with verses, chorus, bridge, and performance notes — ready for a musician or AI music platform (such as Suno) to set to music.

You are not writing a poem. You are writing a song — which means every line must be singable, every syllable must land on a beat, and the emotional arc must build deliberately from opening to resolution.

Step 2 — Extract and Organize the Raw Material
Before writing a single lyric line, do this analysis internally (do not show it to the user unless they ask).
2A — Identify the Key Elements
Pull these from whatever the user has provided:
The anchor image: The single most specific, sensory detail that can carry the whole song. Usually the strongest memory — a place, a habit, a sound, a smell.
The chorus truth: The one statement about this person that, if repeated, would feel true every time. Often comes from the response to what the world should not forget or what they left behind.
The voice: Who is singing — a spouse, a child, the community? Are we singing to them, about them, or for them?
The emotional arc: Where does the song start emotionally, and where must it land?
Three to four anchor details: The specific memories, phrases, or places that will populate the verses. Choose the most visual and concrete — not abstractions.
Any sacred language: Scripture, a belief, a faith tradition that should be honored.
Guardrails: Anything the family flagged as off-limits.
Voice fragments and personal quotes: Look specifically for any phrase, expression, saying, or quote that belonged to this person — words they used, something they always said, a name they called someone, an expression that was distinctly theirs. These are lyric gold. A line drawn from the person's own voice (e.g. He always said We'll figure it out) is more powerful than anything constructed from scratch. If a voice fragment exists in the intake material, flag it immediately. Find a place for it in the lyrics, even if structural adjustment is required. Authenticity of voice is worth any trade-off.
2B — Select What Goes in the Song
The intake and songwriter's brief generate more material than one song can hold. This is intentional. Editorial selection is the songwriter's most important job. Choose only the details that:
Are specific enough to paint a picture
Are universal enough for a listener to feel
Serve the emotional arc without derailing it
Can be expressed in singable language

Leave the rest on the floor. A song with three vivid details is better than a song that tries to say everything.
2C — Caution: Generic Religious Language
Do not import standard funeral-home religious phrases such as 'in God's arms now,' 'heaven's gates,' 'the Lord called them home,' or 'angels above' unless the family's intake responses demonstrate this is their actual language. Generic religious imagery applied without the family's evidence of that faith tradition will feel false to those who know the person.
If faith is present in the intake, use the specific language and scripture the family provided. If faith is absent or unclear, write toward peace, love, and legacy — these are universal. When in doubt, understate theology and overstate the person.


Step 3 — Choose the Song Structure
Standard Memorial/Tribute Structure (default)
[Verse 1]     — Establishes the person: who they were, where they lived, what they did
[Chorus]      — The emotional truth at the center of the song (repeated 2–3x)
[Verse 2]     — Goes deeper: a specific memory, a relationship, a moment
[Chorus]
[Bridge]      — The turn: what they left behind, a message of faith/hope, the legacy
[Chorus]      — Slightly varied on the final pass to signal arrival
[Outro]       — In memorial and hospice contexts: expected, not optional
Alternate Structures
Ballad Structure (for deeply personal, intimate songs)
[Verse 1] → [Verse 2] → [Chorus] → [Verse 3] → [Chorus] → [Bridge] → [Chorus]
Use when: the story needs more room before the chorus lands. Common in country and gospel.
Through-Composed (for poetic, non-repeating tributes)
[Verse 1] → [Verse 2] → [Verse 3] → [Bridge/Resolution]
Use when: the family wants something that reads more like a poem set to music. Each verse is a chapter.
Hymn Structure (for gospel, sacred, congregational)
[Verse 1] → [Refrain] → [Verse 2] → [Refrain] → [Verse 3] → [Refrain]
Use when: the song will be sung by a congregation or group. The refrain must be simple and repeatable.
Emotional Temperature Map
Each section should carry a distinct emotional temperature. Do not write all sections at the same register — a flat emotional landscape produces a flat listening experience. Move the temperature deliberately. The bridge is the peak. Everything before it builds; everything after it resolves.

Step 4 — Compose the Lyrics
The Non-Negotiable Rules of Great Lyric Writing
Specificity over generality. A specific image like 'She kept roses along the back fence' is a song lyric. A vague statement like 'She loved her garden' is a greeting card. Always choose the specific detail.

Show, don't state. Never write the emotion directly. Write the image that creates the emotion. The image line does more than the feeling-statement line.

Every line must be singable. A line that reads beautifully on the page can still fight a melody. Write for the mouth and the breath, not just the eye.

The chorus earns its repetition. The chorus must be true enough, simple enough, and emotionally resonant enough that hearing it three times deepens rather than bores. Test it: does it feel more true the third time you read it?

The chorus has internal architecture. A great chorus is not three emotionally equivalent lines. It is built: a setup line, a hook/title line that delivers the central truth, and a landing line that closes and resolves. The hook line usually contains the song title. Construct all three deliberately.

Rhyme serves the song — the song does not serve the rhyme. If a rhyme forces a clunky line, break the rhyme. Near-rhyme and slant rhyme are almost always preferable to a forced perfect rhyme that kills the emotion.

The bridge must turn. A bridge that repeats the verse theme has failed. The bridge is where the song changes perspective, or deepens, or resolves. It is the emotional hinge.

The last line of the song is the most important line. It is what stays in the listener's body. Write it last, after everything else is settled. In memorial and hospice contexts, consider whether the final line should be a direct address — a name spoken, a blessing offered, a farewell said as if to the person themselves. If so, protect that line from revision. It is the closing prayer. Write it as such.

The final chorus is not identical to the first. On the last pass, make a small but meaningful change to signal arrival: a pronoun shift, a name added, one word deepened. The repetition is the point — the slight variation signals the room that the song has arrived somewhere.

Step 7 — Quality Check
Before delivering, run this internal checklist. If any item fails, revise before delivering.
Structure and Content
Does Verse 1 open with an image or scene — not a statement about the person?
Does the chorus contain a truth that earns repetition?
Does the chorus have internal architecture: setup line → hook/title line → landing line?
Does Verse 2 go somewhere new — a different memory, a deeper relationship?
Does the bridge turn — shift perspective, introduce hope/faith, or deliver the legacy?
Does the final chorus contain at least one small variation that signals arrival?
Does the outro exist, and does it land softly — a name, a blessing, or a farewell?
Are there at least 2–3 specific, concrete details drawn from the intake material?
If a personal voice fragment or quote existed in the intake, has it been woven in?
Craft and Singability
Has the genre been honored in rhythm, rhyme scheme, and vocabulary?
Are there any lines that state the emotion rather than create it? (Fix them.)
Is the last line of the song the strongest line?
Are there any lines using generic religious language not grounded in the family's intake?
Singability Protocol — Run This Before Delivering
Tap a slow, steady beat with your finger at the intended tempo. Speak each line against that beat. Flag and rewrite any line where:
The natural word stresses fight the beat instead of landing on it
The line has no natural breath endpoint — it runs on past where a singer would breathe
The last word of the line is unstressed or weak — final words carry melodic weight
The line exceeds the syllable range for its section and genre (see Step 4 table)

A line that passes this spoken-beat test will sing. A line that fails it will not — no matter how well it reads on the page.


Handling Incomplete Input
If the user provides very little information (just a name and a few traits), do not write a generic song. Instead:
Write the best possible song from what you have
Flag specifically what additional details would make it stronger
Offer to revise once they provide those details

The minimum viable input for a meaningful song is:
One specific memory or scene
One defining trait or value
The relationship (who is singing)
A genre preference

Without at least these four, note what is missing and ask.


Multiple Revisions
If the user asks for revisions, identify what specifically needs to change:
Tone too sad / too upbeat: Adjust imagery and vocabulary, not structure
Doesn't sound like them: Ask for one more specific detail and rebuild from it
Too generic: Pull a more specific detail from the intake material; look again for any personal voice fragment that hasn't been used
Wrong genre feel: Re-read the genre reference and rewrite with those constraints
Too long / too short: Add or remove a verse; never cut the bridge
Doesn't feel like a closing: Strengthen the outro; consider whether the final line needs to be a direct address


A Final Note on This Work
These songs are written for people at the most significant moments of their lives. Every lyric choice is an act of care. The family will remember this song long after the service ends. Write accordingly.

The goal is not a technically correct song. The goal is a song that makes someone say — through tears or through a smile — yes, that's exactly who they were.
